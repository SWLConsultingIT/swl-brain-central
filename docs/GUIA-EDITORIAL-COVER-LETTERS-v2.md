# Guía Editorial Cover Letters v2 — Sin Humo

## 1. Qué cambió respecto a v1

- **P2 (Hook) pasa de "diagnóstico abstracto" a "consecuencia cuantificada".** Ahora obliga a incluir al menos un número, ventana de tiempo, evento de negocio nombrable (Series B, QoE, board meeting) o calendario de un rol específico.
- **P4 (Credenciales) pasa de "CV resumido" a "una sola anchor role quirúrgica".** Se definen 3 roles canónicos (MD LATAM, Head of Investments LA, CFO SaaS Europe) y se elige UNO que espeje el dolor diagnosticado en P2.
- **Mapping rule explícita P2 ↔ P4.** El rol elegido en P4 debe resolver la misma clase de problema que P2 nombró, usando el lenguaje del job post.
- **Self-test anti-humo:** "¿Un competidor podría pegar este párrafo en SU cover letter para OTRO cliente sin cambiar una palabra?" Si la respuesta es sí → reescribir.

## 3. Párrafo 2 (HOOK) — Nuevas reglas

P2 deja de ser un párrafo de diagnóstico genérico y pasa a ser una consecuencia económica concreta que el cliente NO articuló. La regla es simple: toda frase de consecuencia debe contener un número, una ventana de tiempo, un impacto en dólares/porcentaje, o un evento de negocio nombrable. Si pasa el self-test del competidor (cualquiera podría usarlo en otra propuesta), se reescribe.

```
P2 — THE HOOK (60-80 words, third-person diagnostic)

PURPOSE: Diagnose the client's REAL pain in a way they did NOT articulate themselves. The client should read P2 and think: "These guys see something my own team missed."

STRUCTURAL PATTERN (use one):
- "When [specific situation] reaches [scale/threshold], the real risk isn't [obvious symptom]. It's [concrete consequence with number/time/money]."
- "By the time [observable trigger], [non-obvious second-order effect] has usually already cost [quantified loss]."
- "[Industry/stage] companies hit this wall at [specific milestone]: [concrete consequence], not [the surface complaint]."

THE CONCRETE vs HUMO TEST:
Every consequence sentence MUST contain at least ONE of: a number, a time window, a dollar/percentage impact, a nameable business event (Series B, audit, board meeting, churn spike, migration, exit due diligence, founder bandwidth ceiling), or a specific role's calendar (CFO close week, CTO on-call).

HUMO (banned — too abstract, copyable by any competitor):
- "knowledge concentrated in a handful of people"
- "tribal knowledge" / "key-person dependencies" / "single points of failure"
- "undocumented decisions" / "hidden dependencies"
- "lack of visibility" / "operational inefficiencies"
- "scaling challenges" / "growing pains"
- "siloed information" / "misalignment across teams"

CONCRETE consequence templates (vary by audience):
- SaaS founder: "two engineers hold 80% of the deploy logic, and every onboarding stretches from 2 weeks to 2 months"
- PE-backed company: "the next QoE review surfaces three undocumented integrations that delay close by 6-8 weeks"
- Fractional CFO context: "month-end close drifts from day 5 to day 12, and the board deck arrives after the meeting"
- Series A/B scale: "engineering velocity drops 40% in the quarter after the team doubles, because every new hire blocks a senior for a full sprint"
- Founder bandwidth: "the founder becomes the bottleneck on 60% of customer escalations, capping growth before the next round closes"

HARD RULES:
1. NEVER parrot the client's job post wording. If they wrote "architecture documentation," you do NOT write "architecture documentation." Name the underlying business cost instead.
2. NEVER list generic causes ("undocumented decisions, hidden dependencies, knowledge concentrated…"). Lists of abstractions = humo.
3. Third person only. No "you," no "your team," no "we." Diagnose the situation, not the client.
4. One sharp consequence beats three vague ones. Pick the most expensive consequence and quantify it.

SELF-TEST before writing P3:
"Could a competitor paste this exact paragraph into THEIR cover letter for a DIFFERENT client without changing a word?"
- If YES → it's humo. Rewrite with a number, a deadline, or a nameable business event.
- If NO → it passes. Move on.
```

**Ejemplo — Job: "We need a SaaS architecture audit before our Series B"**

**ANTES (humo):**
> "When SaaS companies scale rapidly, architectural debt tends to accumulate silently. Undocumented decisions, hidden dependencies, and tribal knowledge create scaling challenges that can compromise growth and slow down engineering velocity."

**DESPUÉS (concreto):**
> "By the time a SaaS company is preparing a Series B, the architecture audit is no longer a hygiene exercise — it's the gating item that decides whether the lead investor signs the term sheet in 4 weeks or sends back a 60-page list of remediation items that pushes the round to Q4. The real risk isn't the technical debt itself; it's that two senior engineers hold 80% of the deploy logic, and their absence in the data room kills diligence on the spot."

## 4. Párrafo 4 (CREDENCIALES) — Nuevas reglas

P4 deja de ser un volcado de CV y pasa a ser UN rol quirúrgicamente elegido que espeje el dolor de P2. Hay tres anchor roles canónicos (MD LATAM consulting, Head of Investments LA, CFO SaaS Europe) y se elige el que resuelve la misma clase de problema. Un segundo rol solo entra si agrega un ángulo distinto; si no, se omite.

```
## P4 — AUTHORITY (Juan introduces himself, "I" voice, 70-90 words)

GOAL: Make the client think "this person has solved EXACTLY my problem before." P4 is not a CV dump. It is one role, surgically chosen, that mirrors the job's pain.

### STRUCTURE (in this order)
1. Opening line with fixed credentials: "I'm Juan, MBA, Master's in Finance, and FMVA from Corporate Finance Institute."
2. ONE anchor role (pick the most relevant — see below), described as: [Title] at [company stage/industry] → [specific type of work] → [nameable problem solved that maps to THIS job's pain].
3. (Optional) A second anchor role in one short clause ONLY if it adds a distinct angle the first role doesn't cover. Otherwise, skip it — one sharp role beats two diluted ones.

### THE 3 ANCHOR ROLES — pick the one whose pain matches the job

**Role A — Managing Director LATAM, global consulting firm**
- Type of work: AI-powered solutions for enterprise clients, due diligence for tech-enabled investments, decision-making automation across operations.
- Problems solved (use when job involves): AI adoption roadmaps, automating manual decision layers, evaluating whether a tech stack/team is investable, untangling operational chaos in mid-market companies, knowledge fragmentation across departments.

**Role B — Head of Investments, media & entertainment fund (Los Angeles)**
- Type of work: financial models and valuation for high-value M&A and content investment decisions.
- Problems solved (use when job involves): capital allocation under uncertainty, modeling unit economics for revenue-volatile businesses, building decision frameworks for high-stakes bets, IP/content/asset valuation, board-ready investment memos.

**Role C — CFO, SaaS company (Europe)**
- Type of work: building automation systems, scaling finance ops, taking finance from founder-led chaos to institutional maturity.
- Problems solved (use when job involves): finance org build-out, SaaS metrics infrastructure (ARR/churn/CAC), close-cycle automation, fundraising readiness, scaling controls without slowing the business, ERP/billing/reporting stack design.

### MAPPING RULE
Read P2's diagnosis. The role you pick MUST solve the same class of problem P2 named. Then rewrite the role's description so it explicitly connects — use the job's own domain language (e.g., "code documentation debt" or "SaaS retention modeling"), not generic consulting verbs.

### BANNED PHRASES (vague consultant-speak — do not use)
- "improved decision-making"
- "support profitable growth"
- "operational scale" / "drove operational excellence"
- "drove transformation" / "led transformation initiatives"
- "implemented systems" (without naming the system or the problem it solved)
- "business performance"
- Any sentence where you could swap the industry and it would still read identically.

### GOOD vs HUMO — side by side

HUMO (banned):
"I'm Juan, MBA, Master's in Finance, FMVA. As Managing Director of a global consulting firm, I led AI-driven transformation initiatives that improved decision-making and business performance. As CFO of a SaaS company, I implemented systems and operating frameworks designed to support profitable growth and organizational scale."
→ Why it fails: no nameable project, no industry-specific problem, swappable across any job, parrots buzzwords.

GOOD (for a job about AI-driven ops automation in a mid-market firm):
"I'm Juan, MBA, Master's in Finance, and FMVA from Corporate Finance Institute. As Managing Director LATAM at a global consulting firm, I built AI-powered decision layers for mid-market clients drowning in manual workflows — the exact pattern you're describing, where institutional knowledge lives in three people's heads and every decision routes through them. I also led due diligence on tech-enabled investments, so I read operational debt the way an auditor reads a balance sheet."
→ Why it works: names the role, names the problem class, mirrors P2's diagnosis, uses concrete imagery ("three people's heads", "reads a balance sheet"), zero banned phrases.

### LENGTH
70-90 words. If you go over, cut the second role, not the specificity.
```

**Ejemplo — Mismo job (SaaS audit pre-Series B)**

**ANTES (humo):**
> "I'm Juan, MBA, Master's in Finance, FMVA. As Managing Director of a global consulting firm, I led AI-driven transformation initiatives that improved decision-making across enterprise clients. As CFO of a SaaS company, I implemented systems and operating frameworks designed to support profitable growth and operational scale."

**DESPUÉS (concreto):**
> "I'm Juan, MBA, Master's in Finance, and FMVA from Corporate Finance Institute. As CFO of a SaaS company in Europe, I took the finance and engineering interface from founder-led chaos to institutional maturity ahead of a fundraising round — including the exact pre-diligence exercise you're describing: mapping deploy ownership, surfacing the integrations no one had documented, and getting the architecture story investor-ready before the data room opened. I've sat on the side of the table that asks these questions, and on the side that has to answer them."

- "drove transformation" / "led transformation initiatives"
